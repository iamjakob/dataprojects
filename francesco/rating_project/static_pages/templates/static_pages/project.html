The dataset

I am using datasets from Yelp, consisting of about 600,000 reviews. The test set, which contains 23,000 reviews, is not used during feature selection and model training.

Metrics

  • Accuracy (or number of correctly identified ratings)
  • Mean absolute and squared error
  • One-away ratio (reviews predicted at most 1 away from the ground truth)

First models

My first quick classifier was a Naive Bayes on bag of words, selected by frequency. Accuracy between 50 and 54%, doesn't improve much increasing size of the bag of words.

Negations

Misclassified reviews revealed the need of better treatmen of negations. I decided to add a preprocessing step and attach every occurrence of not to the following word. For example,

not great

was transformed into

not_great

N-grams

To get past the accuracy wall of the previous method, I employed higher order n-grams. This time, instead of selecting by frequency across the entire corpus, I subdivided the reviews into three categories:

  • negative (1 or 2 stars)
  • neutral (3 stars)
  • positive (4 or 5 stars)

and selected frequent N-grams in each category. I then merged the sets of n-grams before feeding them to the vectorizer. (I did this because the training set contains relatively few negative reviews; as a consequence selecting by frequency would yield a larger amount of features from positive reviews. This reflected in the results of early classifiers, where accuracy on negative reviews was significantly lower than that on positive ones.)

Results

Here are the results for Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression classifiers. Logistic regression performs better on all fronts, achieving an accuracy of 63.7%, and predicting at within 1 from the ground truth in 93.8% of the cases. Curiously Logistic Regression tends to overshoot compared to the naive Bayes classifier.
